# Competitive Positioning

This document defines how Scope is positioned relative to alternatives.

It is not for the homepage. It informs marketing copy, landing pages, comparison content, and internal decision-making about what to build and what to ignore.

---

## Positioning Statement

Scope is a client work lifecycle system for freelancers, consultants, and small studios.

It replaces the duct-tape stack — Toggl + Wave + ClickUp + Google Docs + email — with a single system where time, tasks, proposals, contracts, and billing are connected by default.

It replaces maximalist platforms like Bonsai and HoneyBook with an opinionated, calm alternative that doesn't try to automate your judgment or grow past your needs.

---

## The Real Competition

Scope's primary competitor is not a single product.

It is the **duct-tape stack**: the combination of 3–5 tools that most freelancers and small teams already use. Each tool is good at its slice. The user is the glue.

Common stacks:
- Toggl + Wave + ClickUp + Google Docs
- Harvest + Notion + Stripe + email
- Clockify + FreshBooks + Asana + spreadsheets

The switching cost is high because each tool is familiar, and the combined cost is often low or free. The pain is diffuse — "everything works, nothing connects" — which makes it easy to tolerate and hard to articulate.

Scope's pitch to this audience: the connections between features are worth more than the individual features themselves. Time that's already linked to a project that's already linked to an agreement that's already linked to an invoice — that chain is the product.

## Direct Competitors

### Bonsai

Closest feature overlap (~70%). YC-backed, 500k+ users, $17–79/mo per seat.

**What they do well:**
- Exists, ships, and has years of edge cases solved
- Templates, contracts, invoicing, time tracking, proposals
- Mobile app, integrations, client portal
- Brand recognition in freelancer space

**Where Scope diverges:**
- Bonsai has no lifecycle concept — projects don't move through phases
- Bonsai automates decisions; Scope supports judgment
- Bonsai uses per-seat pricing; Scope uses flat pricing
- Bonsai is expanding upmarket (agencies, banking, resource planning); Scope stays focused
- Bonsai's positioning: "put your freelancing on autopilot" — the opposite of Scope's philosophy

**Structural difference:**
Bonsai grew by adding features. Scope's bet is that connections between features are the product. That's a fundamentally different architecture decision that's hard to retrofit.

**Who Scope wins:**
People Bonsai is growing away from. Freelancers and small studios who want fewer surprises, not more features. People skeptical of VC-backed tools that expand past their needs.

---

### Harvest + Forecast

$10.80/seat/mo. Calm, mature, boring (positive).

**What they do well:**
- Time tracking + invoicing that works
- Clean, focused, no bloat
- Respectful of users

**Where Scope diverges:**
- Harvest covers a slice, not the lifecycle
- No proposals, contracts, lifecycle phases, or scope tracking
- Per-seat pricing

**Who Scope wins:**
Harvest users who want the same calm energy but need more of the lifecycle covered.

---

### HoneyBook / Dubsado

Cover the client lifecycle but for creative businesses (photographers, event planners, wedding vendors).

**Where Scope diverges:**
- Different lifecycle than dev/consulting work
- Configuration-heavy where Scope is opinionated
- Flashy where Scope is quiet
- HoneyBook is VC-backed with aggressive growth trajectory

**Who Scope wins:**
Technical freelancers and consultants who tried HoneyBook/Dubsado and found it too noisy or too oriented toward creative services.

---

### Wave

Free invoicing, $19/mo Pro. H&R Block acquired 2019.

**Where Scope diverges:**
- Wave is invoicing-first, not lifecycle-aware
- Moved core features behind paywall in 2024
- Customer service problems, payment holds, trust erosion
- Acquisition trajectory is a cautionary tale

**Who Scope wins:**
Wave refugees. People who got burned by an acquisition that changed the product's priorities.

---

### ClickUp / Monday / Asana

Productivity platforms. Opposite of Scope's philosophy.

**Where Scope diverges:**
- These are configurable work OSes
- Require constant maintenance and configuration
- No billing, invoicing, or client lifecycle features
- Optimize for flexibility, which creates the mess Scope prevents

**Who Scope wins:**
People tired of configuring their tools instead of doing their work.

## Scope's Unfair Advantages

### 1. Lifecycle awareness

Projects move through explicit phases. Proposal → agreement → tracked work → invoice is a connected chain. Nobody else does this. Scope creep becomes visible by default — what was proposed vs. what's happening.

This is the hardest thing for competitors to copy because it's an architecture decision, not a feature bolt-on.

### 2. Independence

No VC funding. No growth mandate. No pressure to add enterprise features, banking products, or agency-scale tooling.

Pricing stays flat. Features stay focused. The product doesn't outgrow its users.

For anyone who's watched a tool get acquired, repriced, or pivoted upmarket — this is a signal, not a tagline.

### 3. Measurement vs. billing separation

All work is measured. Not all work is billed. Internal work is first-class. Most competitors either ignore non-billable time or treat it as a footnote.

This gives users honest data about where effort actually goes — not just what gets invoiced.

### 4. Flat pricing

No per-seat charges. For a 3-person studio, Bonsai costs $51–237/mo. Scope costs a flat rate regardless of team size.

This changes who you're willing to add to the system.

### 5. Opinionated defaults

Structure beats flexibility. No configurable work OS. No performance tracking, utilization targets, or enterprise features. The product makes decisions so users don't have to.

## The Switching Problem

The audience that values calm tools is also the least likely to enjoy migrating their entire workflow.

The payoff of switching to Scope is diffuse — "everything feels slightly better, nothing falls through cracks" — rather than discrete — "this one thing is now possible."

This means:
- Scope must be immediately understandable in a screenshot or 30-second demo
- The lifecycle chain must be visible, not just architecturally true
- Scope creep visibility is the killer demo moment
- Onboarding must respect the user's existing data and habits

## Positioning by Audience Segment

### Freelancers currently using duct-tape stacks
**Message:** Stop being the glue between your tools.
**Proof point:** Time entry → invoice in one system, no copy-paste.

### Small studios (2–5 people) on Bonsai
**Message:** Same features, calmer approach, flat pricing.
**Proof point:** Add your whole team without tripling your bill.

### Consultants using Harvest + Google Docs
**Message:** The calm you like from Harvest, but covering the full lifecycle.
**Proof point:** Proposals, contracts, and billing in the same system as time tracking.

### Anyone burned by tool acquisitions (Wave, etc.)
**Message:** Independently built. Stays focused. Won't outgrow you.
**Proof point:** No VC. Flat pricing. No feature gating.

---

## What Not to Say

- Don't call Scope "all-in-one" — that implies feature accumulation
- Don't compare feature-for-feature with Bonsai — Scope wins on connections, not checkboxes
- Don't disparage competitors by name on the homepage
- Don't promise "the last tool you'll ever need" — that's hype
- Don't lead with independence as the primary pitch — it's a trust signal, not a value proposition

---

## Summary

Scope's competitive position is not "more features" or "better UI."

It is: **lifecycle awareness + opinionated defaults + flat pricing + independence.**

That combination doesn't exist elsewhere. Bonsai covers features but misses philosophy. Harvest is calm but only covers a slice. HoneyBook covers lifecycle but is noisy and VC-driven.

The gap is real. The challenge is making it visible before someone reads the manifesto.
